France • Laïcité • Security State • Muslim Citizenship

When neutrality becomes a border.

This project argues that Islamophobia in France does not operate only through private prejudice or far-right speech. It is produced through a republican language of neutrality that turns visible Muslim life—especially the veil, Muslim associations, Islamic schooling, mosque funding, and security discourse—into a recurring test of belonging.

Core thesis

In France, Islamophobia works by translating racial and religious suspicion into the language of secularism, public order, and national cohesion.

Country profile

France’s Muslim question is inseparable from empire, migration, and the republic.

France is a secular republic with a long colonial history in Muslim-majority regions, especially North and West Africa. Today, many French Muslims are citizens whose families trace their roots to Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia, Turkey, sub-Saharan Africa, and France’s overseas territories. Because France generally avoids official religious statistics in its census, population figures are usually based on surveys and estimates rather than a religious census.

History

Empire to migration

French rule in Algeria, Tunisia, Morocco, parts of West Africa, and the Levant shaped the migration routes that later produced France’s large Muslim population. Postwar labor recruitment, decolonization, family reunification, and asylum flows all made Islam a domestic French reality rather than a foreign issue.

Religious diversity

A secularizing society

INSEE found that in 2019–2020, 51% of people aged 18–59 in metropolitan France said they had no religion; 29% identified as Catholic, 10% as Muslim, and 10% as another religion.2 Islam is therefore both France’s second-largest religion and a frequent target of public controversy.

Political system

Laïcité as identity

Laïcité is formally about state neutrality toward religion. In practice, debates over Muslim clothing, halal food, mosque funding, and Islamic schools often transform neutrality into a demand that Muslims make religion less visible in public life.

Analytical framing

The central pattern: Islamophobia is legalized as “neutrality” and normalized as “security.”

France’s model is not simply “anti-Muslim hatred.” It is a layered system: legal restrictions define certain Muslim practices as incompatible with public neutrality; political rhetoric presents Islam as a threat to national cohesion; media controversies repeatedly reduce Muslims to veils, violence, and integration; and security policies give the state broad power over mosques, schools, associations, and activists.

Legal restrictions

The law targets symbols, but the social target is often Muslim visibility.

Most French restrictions are written in universal language: “conspicuous religious symbols,” “public order,” “neutrality,” “Republican values.” The effect, however, falls heavily on Muslims because the most contested symbol is usually the hijab, abaya, niqab, or qamis.

Electoral politics

Islamophobia is useful because it lets politicians argue about race without saying race.

French political rhetoric often turns Islam into a proxy for immigration, security, gender equality, and national decline. This allows politicians to present restrictive policies as defense of secularism or women’s rights while mobilizing voters around anxiety toward Muslims.

Far right

Marine Le Pen / National Rally

Le Pen’s 2022 campaign included a proposed public-space hijab ban, later softened by allies as the second round approached.6 The political function was clear: the veil became a shorthand for national identity, immigration, and cultural threat.

Far right media-politics

Éric Zemmour

Zemmour’s rhetoric linked Muslims, immigration, and invasion; Reuters reported that France’s top court ordered a retrial in a case involving his anti-Muslim remarks about people wearing traditional religious clothing.7

Mainstream state politics

Gabriel Attal / Macron governments

The abaya ban was announced by then-Education Minister Gabriel Attal and defended as a secularism measure.8 This matters because Islamophobic policy does not only come from the far right; mainstream republican actors also reproduce suspicion toward Muslim visibility.

Media, film, and images

The Muslim is repeatedly made visible as a problem.

French media controversies often focus on visual symbols: veils, abayas, halal signs, mosques, protests, and images of “banlieue” disorder. The repetition matters: even when coverage is framed as debate, it trains audiences to associate Muslim visibility with national crisis.

Visual grammar of Islamophobia

Media discussions often turn Muslim women into symbolic battlegrounds. The hijab or abaya becomes less a garment than a screen onto which anxieties about immigration, gender, secularism, and extremism are projected.

Economic restrictions

Economic Islamophobia works through exclusion, debanking, and professional pressure.

The most visible legal debates are about clothing, but the economic consequences are broader: Muslim women face employment barriers; Muslim associations report difficulty with bank access and funding; and security-linked administrative controls can disrupt schools, mosques, publishers, and charities.

Employment

Hijab and workplace exclusion

Anti-discrimination groups have long documented how visible Muslim women face hiring barriers and pressure to remove religious dress. This turns a public debate about secularism into private economic exclusion.

Banking

Association financing

Reports and official dialogue around the Forum de l’Islam de France show that Muslim religious associations have faced problems in relations with banks; in 2025, the Interior Minister promised dialogue on banking access for Muslim associations.9

State pressure

Funding and closures

Reuters reported that France’s campaign against “separatism” affected Muslim schools, including the loss of state funding for Averroès, France’s largest Muslim private school.10 Economic pressure therefore becomes a tool of ideological governance.

Surveillance and emergency powers

The security state makes Muslim institutions governable before they are proven guilty.

After terrorist attacks, France expanded administrative and security powers. Some measures address real threats, but civil liberties groups argue that the architecture of prevention can cast suspicion over Muslim religious life as a whole.

2015–2017: emergency logic

After major attacks, emergency practices normalized administrative searches, closures, and security measures. The danger is not that security is irrelevant, but that exceptional tools can become ordinary governance.

2020–2021: association dissolutions

The French government dissolved the Collective Against Islamophobia in France; Human Rights Watch argued that the Council of State’s approval damaged freedom of association and expression.11

2022: mosque closures

Reuters reported that French authorities had closed 22 mosques over 18 months, with critics objecting to secretive evidence and limited procedural transparency.12

2023–2025: normalization of oversight

Human Rights Watch warned that the 2021 separatism framework threatened freedom of association, while later reports noted wider constraints on civil society and rising hate crime indicators.13

Countering Islamophobia

Resistance comes from legal advocacy, women’s sports campaigns, civil liberties work, and local Muslim organizing.

Positive examples matter because they keep the project from becoming only a story of victimization. French Muslims and allies have challenged Islamophobia through litigation, documentation, public campaigns, academic reports, and alternative institutions.

Civil society

CCIE / successor advocacy

After the CCIF dissolution, anti-Islamophobia advocacy continued through European and local networks, including documentation, victim support, and public reporting.

Sports campaigns

Basket Pour Toutes

Campaigns led by Muslim women athletes challenged hijab restrictions in basketball and other sports, reframing the issue as equal participation rather than “religious separatism.”

Institutional dialogue

FORIF / ADDAM

The Forum de l’Islam de France and ADDAM were presented by the Interior Ministry as efforts to address Muslim chaplaincy, cemetery needs, bank access, and anti-Muslim acts, though representativeness remains debated.9

Positive cases

Success does exist—but often despite the system, not because of it.

Examples include legal challenges to discriminatory policies, Muslim women’s athletic organizing, public documentation of anti-Muslim acts, interfaith coalitions, and local initiatives that protect mosque security or expand chaplaincy. The strongest positive framing is not “France is simply improving,” but “organized resistance forces the republic to live up to its own promises.”
