Secularism, Security, and the Muslim Question in France

This project argues that Islamophobia in France does not operate only as individual prejudice. It is structured through the language of republican secularism, public order, national security, and cultural assimilation, turning Muslim visibility into a recurring test of whether Muslims can fully belong.

Central argument

Islamophobia as a system of conditional citizenship

In the French case, Islamophobia works by defining Muslims as citizens whose belonging is conditional: they may be included as individuals, but visible Muslim practices are repeatedly framed as threats to secularism, women’s rights, national cohesion, or public security. This creates a contradiction at the heart of the republic: universal equality is claimed in principle, while Muslim difference is treated as a problem to be regulated.

Brief history

France’s relationship with Islam is inseparable from empire. French colonial rule in Algeria, Tunisia, Morocco, West Africa, and the Levant made Muslims subjects of French power long before many became citizens or residents inside metropolitan France. Postwar labor migration, family reunification, and the descendants of immigrants transformed Islam into France’s second-largest religion.

Religious diversity

France is officially secular and does not organize citizenship through religious categories. Yet public debates often focus intensely on Islam, especially Muslim dress, mosques, halal markets, Islamic schools, migration, and “separatism.” This means Muslims are both officially invisible as a religious minority and highly visible as a political problem.

Legal restrictions

When neutrality becomes regulation of Muslim visibility

2004
Public school religious symbols ban.

The law banning conspicuous religious symbols in public schools applied to multiple religions, but its political target was widely understood to include Muslim girls wearing headscarves.

2010
Full-face veil ban.

France banned face coverings in public space, affecting Muslim women wearing niqab and burqa. The state framed the measure around public order, security, and republican values.

2021
“Separatism” law.

The law strengthened oversight over religious associations, homeschooling, foreign funding, and organizations accused of undermining republican values. Critics argue that even where the text is formally neutral, the political context makes Muslims the main object of suspicion.

2023
Abaya and qamis school ban.

France’s education minister banned abayas and qamis in public schools, and the Conseil d’État upheld the measure. Supporters framed it as secular neutrality; critics saw it as another expansion of state control over Muslim-coded clothing.

Electoral politics

Political Islamophobia as campaign infrastructure

Far-right normalization

Figures such as Marine Le Pen and Éric Zemmour have repeatedly connected Islam, immigration, crime, and national decline. Their rhetoric pressures mainstream parties to adopt harsher language on secularism, borders, and “separatism.” Islamophobia therefore works not only through explicitly far-right discourse but also through agenda-setting: it moves the center of debate.

Mainstream adoption

Presidents and ministers often defend restrictive policy through republican universalism rather than openly anti-Muslim language. This makes French Islamophobia distinctive: it frequently appears as a defense of equality, women’s rights, or neutrality while disproportionately regulating Muslim practices.

Analytical claim: the key political move is translation. Muslim practices are translated into problems of secularism, security, or integration, allowing discriminatory outcomes to be defended as universal republican policy.
Media, film, and images

The Muslim body as a visual symbol of crisis

Headscarf debates

French media coverage often turns Muslim women’s clothing into evidence in larger debates about freedom, patriarchy, extremism, immigration, and national identity. This creates a narrow visual vocabulary where Muslim women are made to stand in for the “problem” of Islam in France.

Security framing

After terrorist attacks, media narratives often blur distinctions between violent groups, ordinary religious conservatism, immigration, and Muslim civil society. This does not mean all security concerns are invented; it means the burden of suspicion is distributed unevenly across Muslim communities.

Economic restrictions

Discrimination through work, banking, travel, and opportunity

Employment

Muslims and people perceived as Muslim often face discrimination in hiring, especially when names, addresses, clothing, or racialized markers signal North African, African, Arab, Turkish, or Muslim background.

Banking and business

Islamophobia can appear indirectly through “risk” systems: account closures, extra scrutiny of charities, difficulty moving money internationally, or suspicion toward Muslim civic and religious organizations.

Travel and borders

Muslim citizens, immigrants, refugees, and travelers from Muslim-majority states may face intensified scrutiny through visa systems, watchlists, airport questioning, or administrative suspicion.

Surveillance and emergency powers

The security state and the expansion of suspicion

Emergency logic

Counterterrorism policies after major attacks normalized expanded surveillance, administrative closures, raids, and exceptional measures. Even when formally justified by security, these policies often created collective pressure on Muslim spaces such as mosques, schools, charities, and community associations.

Associational pressure

The dissolution of the Collective Against Islamophobia in France (CCIF) became a major example for critics who argue that anti-racist Muslim civil society itself can be treated as suspicious when it challenges state policy.

Counter-organizations and positive cases

Muslim civil society, rights groups, and legal resistance

Rights organizations

Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, the Open Society Justice Initiative, and other organizations have documented concerns over religious freedom, association, and discriminatory impact.

Community organizations

Muslim organizations, student groups, mosque networks, and anti-racist collectives continue to document discrimination, challenge bans, support affected families, and provide legal advocacy.

Positive examples

French Muslims have shaped public life as scholars, athletes, artists, entrepreneurs, doctors, lawyers, teachers, and public servants. Their presence challenges the narrative that Islam is foreign to France.

Conclusion

What the French case reveals

France shows how Islamophobia can be embedded in liberal and republican language rather than only in openly racist speech. The country’s case is therefore especially important: it demonstrates how a state can promise universal equality while repeatedly making Muslim visibility into an object of law, media spectacle, electoral mobilization, and security management.
